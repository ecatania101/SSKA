Our History, Ourselves

Background Information for the Teacher

American Indians: Many Nations is a visually rich nonfiction book that introduces students to the diversity, history, and resilience of American Indian tribal nations. Organized thematically, the book emphasizes that American Indians are not a single group but many distinct nations with their own languages, traditions, and ways of life. From the Great Plains to the Pacific Northwest, the book describes how geography and resources shaped communities and how these cultures adapted over time while preserving key traditions. Students learn how American Indians lived historically—through farming, hunting, storytelling, and crafts—and how many of these practices continue today in both traditional and modern forms.

A strong thread throughout the text is cultural continuity and pride. The book includes real-world examples of traditions, such as basket weaving, totem carving, and storytelling, and profiles leaders and heroes like Sitting Bull. It highlights how tribal nations maintain their identities through language, dance, powwows, and daily life. A timeline from 15,000 years ago to the present day supports historical understanding, while callout sections like offer hands-on or reflective learning opportunities. The closing activity invites students to reflect on their own family traditions, fostering personal connection and cultural awareness.
